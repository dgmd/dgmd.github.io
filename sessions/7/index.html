Making just to make

Warning Signs

  • You sit down to work, and you consistently find yourself distracted.
  • You don't know anyone personally who would benefit from what you want to make.
  • You think that a "class project" (or "app" or "art project") has to look or sound a certain way and so reject ideas of yours before they have a chance to grow.
  • You want to want to do your project.

Solutions

  • Schedule a time to sit down with one of us to brainstorm your project, interests, and goals for the course!
  • Take one-off ideas and interests which pique you seriously, even if they don't seem like a "good project."
  • Don't worry about the relative technical complexity of the project. In our experience, doing anything real is hard enough, and even small tweaks can incur plenty of technical work, if that's a priority for you—

Planning instead of growing

Warning Signs

  • Because it's uncomfortable to not know things (much less ask for help), you instead spend more time planning, imagining, worrying about problems two- and three- and four steps ahead.
  • You say to yourself, "I need to learn more about X before I start."
  • You can't point to a few explorations of making small things or attempts toward sculpting your initial prototype.

Solutions

  • Tell yourself that you're going to throw this piece away, but that you're going to build it to either test out a piece of your project or learn how to do part of your project. Not everything needs to make it into the final cut.
  • If you're not sure how to figure out which piece that is (or how to work on just one piece), ask us!
  • Figure out a regular practice (of sharing mockups, )

Not getting stuck

Warning Signs

  • You don't have code in your journal or sitting on your computer somewhere which didn't work.
  • You're not sure what the next is, but you wouldn't say you're stuck.
  • You haven't emailed looking for help.
  • You haven't ended a session trying to get something work for a few hours frustrated. Or you haven't worked through a frustration after ending a session working frustrated.

Solutions

  • Sit down and articulate a milestone for the next week (or next day). Email that to us. When your deadline comes around, just share what you've tried and the progress you've made (or haven't!)
  • Ask to sit down and just play and explore—we love just sitting down to sketch and explore together, more like a jam session than a tennis lesson.

Letting yourself stay stuck

Warning signs

  • You watch video after video or read page after page about what you're trying to do, but don't feel like you're making progress.
  • You switch between possible solutions before finding out whether one works or not.
  • You've been stuck on the same problem for more than a day or two and haven't reached out.

Solutions

  • Post your question to Stack Overflow— Or at least sit down and write up your question and situation such that a stranger could understand what was going on and try to help.
  • And then maybe post in the DGMD Slack channel! Or email us! Or just ask to get together with us and work through the topic—not even addressing your problem directly.

wrong level of detail

Warning Signs

  • You find yourself drawing things out in pixel perfect detail
  • You find yourself saying, "I'll figure this out later" instead of "I'll do this later"
  • You've drawn something out in sufficient detail to begin using HTML/CSS/JS to prototype what you've drawn, but you spend your time refining the visual details.

Solutions

Hyperfocusing on the tool

Warning Signs

  • You spend more time searching for someone else who's done what you're trying to do than trying to do it yourself.
  • You know what your problem is and that you want a tool to solve it, but you're not sure how you would tackle the problem (meaning the tool isn't "just" saving you effort).
  • You spend more time trying to understand someone else's systme and code rather than the fundamentals of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript

Solutions

  • Sit down with us and ask what portion(s) of your project might best be done by others
  • Ask us for estimates of how much time—given your current skill level—different parts of your project will take.
  • Sit down with these materials

In short, the biggest mistakes are not iterating in a lightweight way, and even more importantly, not letting us help.

We're always available by email, in #Slack, and especially in person.

Sharing out

  2. What's the next question I need answered?
  3. What's the next skill I would really benefit from developing?
